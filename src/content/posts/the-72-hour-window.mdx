Someone just enrolled. Their intent is at its peak. That window is short.

## Why the first 72 hours are different

Enrollment is a commitment act. The moment someone signs up, they've made a small decision: I'm in.

That decision opens a brief window. The context that drove enrollment is still live. The reason is recent. The intent hasn't been displaced yet by everything else competing for attention.

After 72 hours, that changes. The sense of newness fades. What felt relevant on Tuesday feels like admin by Friday.

## The source tells you why

Not all enrollments carry the same intent.

Someone who enrolled at checkout after a promotional prompt is responding to a specific moment of value. Someone who searched for the program and signed up directly is expressing a preference. The channel and trigger say something about motivation.

The first communication should reflect the source. A message calibrated to why someone enrolled is more likely to continue a conversation already in progress. A generic message starts a new one — and often loses.

## What blocks the first transaction

A new member who hasn't transacted isn't disengaged. They may not know what to do next, or not feel enough urgency to do it now.

Three things commonly create friction: they're unclear on what the program offers them specifically, there's no reason to act today rather than later, or the reward threshold feels too distant to matter.

The first communication should address at least one of these. Addressing all three at once is noise.

## The First Value Moment

The FVM is a single, concrete incentive — immediately available after enrollment, designed to make the first transaction feel worth doing now.

It doesn't need to be large. It needs to be proportional to the decision barrier. A high-frequency category with a low transaction threshold needs a smaller push. An infrequent, high-consideration purchase needs more.

The FVM works when it reduces uncertainty and creates a specific reason to act. It doesn't work when it's generic, delayed, or more compelling to a deal-seeker than to a genuine customer.

## The risk of over-communicating

The enrollment window is also the highest-risk period for opt-out.

Too many messages in the first week — welcome email, onboarding sequence, offer, survey — forces the member to make a fast assessment of what membership actually means. If the answer feels like noise, they leave.

An opt-out in week one is rarely about content. It's about frequency and relevance mismatch. One well-timed message outperforms three average ones.

## Finding the right intensity

The optimal FVM sits at a specific point: strong enough to prompt action, not so strong that it distorts behavior.

A stimulus too weak doesn't move the conversion rate. A stimulus too strong attracts members whose primary motivation is the reward — they transact once and don't return.

The signal to look for: activation rate increases without a corresponding drop in second-transaction rate. When both move in the right direction, the FVM is calibrated correctly.

## The First Value Moment framework

Four variables determine whether the FVM lands.

**Source** — What brought them here? The channel and trigger define the intent.

**Barrier** — What's stopping the first transaction? Awareness, urgency, or perceived threshold?

**Stimulus** — What removes that barrier? One thing, proportional to the gap.

**Threshold** — How strong does it need to be? Strong enough to activate. Not so strong it attracts the wrong behavior.

A FVM calibrated to the right barrier for the right enrollment source — that's the 72-hour window used well.
