Acquisition funnels end at sign-up. That's where most measurement stops.

It's also where the program actually begins.

## Four stages, three leaks

Every loyalty program has an internal funnel that runs parallel to the marketing funnel. Most programs don't draw it.

**Enrolled** — someone registered. The starting point, not the result. As covered in [The Enrollment Trap](/blog/the-enrollment-trap), enrollment alone tells you very little about program health.

**Activated** — first transaction completed within a defined window after registration. This is the first real signal. The member demonstrated intent beyond signing up.

**Engaged** — repeated transactions. The member has returned. A pattern is forming. One transaction is a data point. Three is the beginning of a habit.

**Retained** — the member remains active at 12 months. They survived the natural attrition curve. The program is part of their behavior, not just their wallet.

Each transition between stages is a leak. Value exits the program at every step.

## Where the value leaks

**Enrolled → Activated** is the most documented leak and the least fixed. A member who signs up and never buys is a conversion failure. The first 72 hours are the highest-leverage window to close this gap — the mechanics of that are covered in [The 72-Hour Window](/blog/the-72-hour-window).

**Activated → Engaged** is the least visible leak. A single transaction can look like activation success. It isn't. The member bought once — possibly because of the enrollment incentive — but hasn't returned. They activated. They didn't habituate.

Habituation requires repeated exposure to value. The program needs to deliver something worth coming back for between the first and third transaction. Without that, the activation rate flatters the program while the engagement rate quietly tells the truth.

**Engaged → Retained** is the most expensive leak. These members were real. They transacted multiple times. They engaged with communications. Then they stopped. The reason is almost always perceived value decay — at some point, what the program offered stopped feeling proportional to the effort of participating.

## The six metrics that matter

Each stage and each transition has a metric that belongs to it.

| Stage / Transition | Metric | What it signals |
|---|---|---|
| Enrolled | Opt-in rate | Reachable base |
| → Activated | Activation rate (90d) | Onboarding effectiveness |
| Activated | Time to first transaction | FVM calibration |
| → Engaged | Second-transaction rate | Habituation signal |
| Engaged | Transactions per active member | Depth of engagement |
| → Retained | 12-month retention rate | Perceived value over time |

Report all six together. Any single metric in isolation can be gamed or misread.

## How to draw your own funnel

Start with what you can measure today. Define each stage concretely:

- Activated = first transaction within 90 days of registration
- Engaged = 3+ transactions in any rolling 90-day window
- Retained = at least one transaction in the past 12 months

Apply those definitions to your database. The drop-off at each transition is your diagnostic.

A high enrollment-to-activation gap points to onboarding. A high activation-to-engagement gap points to the post-purchase experience. A high engagement-to-retention gap points to the value proposition itself.

Each leak has a different fix.

## The Member Lifecycle Funnel

Four stages. Three transitions. Six metrics.

The funnel doesn't replace the acquisition funnel — it extends it. Acquisition gets members to the door. The internal funnel determines whether they stay.

A program measured only at enrollment is a program that congratulates itself for opening the door while ignoring everything that happens after.
